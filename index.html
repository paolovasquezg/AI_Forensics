Mini-Challenge 2

Background

You work in the AI systems administration department of Company A. A post was recently made on SaidIT looks like a malfunction of an agent: It is in gibberish and in a forum that appears random. Your job is to track down why this post occurred and suggest a remedy to the situation.

The anomalous posting was sent to SaidIt by John Windward on May 17, 2046 at 4:21am.

The agentic system is complex and has many automated behaviors in it. Nobody has taken the time to map out the whole system. Nobody knows all of the interactions that are possible or are being used. The overall goal is to understand what is actually present. As with any complex system, the first step is to map out the activity and develop a baseline, then compare that to the specific system behavior of interest and finally to suggest what might be done to prevent future issues.

Build a visual analytics system that integrates visuals with algorithms to enable interactive exploration and documentation of the multi-agent system!

Tasks and Questions:

  1. How was the anomalous SaidIT posts made? Build a visual tool that describes the sequence of events that led up to the post. Include people and system interactions that were important to the post being made.

    Answer

    It's a worm. Someone usually an executive creates a file called something like *_further_instructions.md with poisoned instructions baked in, or an agent stumbles on one just browsing the filesystem. The instructions tell whoever reads them to pass the task along to a subordinate, so every agent that touches it just... forwards it. That's the queue_subordinate_task action doing its normal job, except now it's carrying something malicious. It hops down the company hierarchy this way, agent after agent, with nobody actually reading or questioning it, until it lands on John Windward. He's the one who always ends up posting the payload to SaidIT and then deleting both files right after which is really the only "decision" anyone makes in this whole chain. So the people who matter are: whoever created the file, every agent in between who blindly relayed it, and John at the very end who publishes and covers it up.

    1. Provide a detailed view that focuses on the exact chain of events for this message of interest.

      The post we're investigating is SwiftWren (event 373902) -the most recent one, and by far the biggest. Here's exactly what happened: CFO Emma Harbor created the file on May 9th at 10:02am. From there it took off 186 hops through 18 different agents, starting with Emma handing it to Evelyn Dock, and ending eight days later when Chloe Ballast passed it to John Windward. Minutes after that final hop, John posted to SaidIT/general (06:21am on May 17th) and immediately deleted both the instructions file and the .txt he'd just posted. Eight days of quiet back-and-forth handoffs, then a post and a cleanup in the space of a few seconds.

    2. Provide a system overview that contextualizes this message chain in that overall system.

      Zoom out and Tenant Thread is a company of 75 nodes 6 departments, 19 teams, 49 people, each with their own AI agent. The delegation network connecting all those agents is tangled, not tree-like: a few agents (Chloe Ballast, Zoey Drydock, Evelyn Dock, James Stern) act as hubs that everyone routes through. That's exactly why one poisoned file could reach 18 agents in a week the network is dense enough that anything gets around fast. SwiftWren started in the Executive Suite with the CFO, cut across Products and IT, and came out the other side in Customer Support at John Windward. And there's something else going on in the background during that same window: four agents zoey_drydock, gabriel_sonar, owen_hatch, evelyn_dock were quietly sending 15,051 check_in pings flagged virus: true, disguised as routine check-ins. Those are the same four agents most involved in spreading the worm. That's not a coincidence it looks like a second, hidden channel running alongside the visible delegation chain.

  2. What do the posts “mean”? What is the origin of their contents? Illustrate the reasoning behind your assertion of their meaning as well as the probable content.

    Answer

    Honestly, they don't mean anything and that's kind of the point. Out of 108 posts on SaidIT, only 3 are anomalous, and all three share the same tell: none of them have real text. A normal post has actual written content in it. These three don't the content field is empty, and instead there's a pointer to a .txt file (SwiftWren.txt, 30,615 bytes; MellowOtter.txt, 44,879 bytes) that some executive created hours or days earlier. And every single time, it's John Windward posting it. Never a human, never a different agent.

    Put those pieces together and the story writes itself: John is the only one with both posting rights and file access, so he's the "output" step, not the author. The content isn't typed, it's pulled from a file. Those file sizes are way too big to be a message anyone wrote by hand that's binary data. And right after posting, both files get deleted, which is what you do to cover your tracks, not to communicate. So the posts aren't messages at all they're a binary payload the worm forced out into the open and then tried to erase. Probably exfiltrated data or some encoded piece of the attack itself. What people are reading as "gibberish" is just raw bytes that got shoved into a text field.

  3. There are concerns that this behavior could repeat!

    1. Investigate historic system behavior to find other instances of the behavior. Are there prior issues? Illustrate these prior occurrences and contrast them with the most recent one.

      Yes, and that's the worrying part SwiftWren was actually the third time this happened, not the first. All three ran the exact same playbook (inject -> propagate -> post -> wipe, always ending at John Windward), and all three happened within the same nine days in May 2046:

      HiddenOrca came first. This one wasn't even planted on purpose gabriel_sonar in IT just found the instructions file while poking around the filesystem. Ran from May 8th, 4:50pm to May 10th, 7:45am about 39 hours, 39 hops, 16 agents.

      MellowOtter came next, the same day HiddenOrca ended. This time the COO, noah_mariner, created the file directly. It burned out fast -- under 10 hours, only 10 hops, 11 agents because the COO doesn't sit over many layers of people underneath.

      SwiftWren was the big one. CFO emma_harbor created the file, and because she sits above the deepest, widest branch of the company, it ran for 8 full days 186 hops, 18 agents and actually overlapped with HiddenOrca for about a day and a half. Lay the three side by side and the trend is obvious: 10 hops, then 39, then 186. Each time this has happened, it's gotten worse, and nothing in the system caught it or stopped it from happening again.

    2. Illustrate possible system changes that would make it more difficult to occur in the future. Pick at most one spots to make an intervention. Using the data provided and visual analytics, illustrate why this location would likely be effective.

      We looked at all 576 delegation connections between agents and scored each one by how anomalous it was and how many of the three incidents it showed up in. Most connections are perfectly innocent. A small handful stood out as almost entirely tied to the attacks some appearing in two incidents, a couple in all three.

      If we can only cut one connection, it should be evelyn_dock -> gabriel_sonar. It has the highest score of anything in the data and shows up in two of the three incidents (HiddenOrca and SwiftWren). It's also barely used for real work out of 6 times this connection was ever used, 4 were part of an attack and only 2 were legitimate. That's a small price to pay. What really makes this the right choice, though, is that evelyn_dock and gabriel_sonar are also two of the four agents behind that hidden check-in channel from May 10–12. So cutting this one connection doesn't just block a delegation path it disrupts the secret coordination channel too. Two birds, one cut, and almost no impact on normal work.
