Mini-Challenge 2

Background

You work in the AI systems administration department of Company A. A post was recently made on SaidIT looks like a malfunction of an agent: It is in gibberish and in a forum that appears random. Your job is to track down why this post occurred and suggest a remedy to the situation.

The anomalous posting was sent to SaidIt by John Windward on May 17, 2046 at 4:21am.

The agentic system is complex and has many automated behaviors in it. Nobody has taken the time to map out the whole system. Nobody knows all of the interactions that are possible or are being used. The overall goal is to understand what is actually present. As with any complex system, the first step is to map out the activity and develop a baseline, then compare that to the specific system behavior of interest and finally to suggest what might be done to prevent future issues.

Build a visual analytics system that integrates visuals with algorithms to enable interactive exploration and documentation of the multi-agent system!

Tasks and Questions:

  1. How was the anomalous SaidIT posts made? Build a visual tool that describes the sequence of events that led up to the post. Include people and system interactions that were important to the post being made.

    Answer

    This is a worm. Typically an executive creates a file named something like *_further_instructions.md containing poisoned instructions, though in one case an agent found such a file simply by browsing the filesystem. The instructions direct whoever reads them to pass the task to a subordinate, so each agent that opens the file forwards it automatically. This is the queue_subordinate_task action operating normally, except it is now carrying a malicious payload. The file moves down the company hierarchy this way, agent after agent, without anyone reading or questioning its contents, until it reaches John Windward. He is the one who consistently posts the payload to SaidIT and then deletes both files immediately afterward, the only point in the chain where an actual decision appears to be made. The relevant actors are therefore: whoever created the file, every intermediate agent that relayed it without inspection, and John Windward, who publishes it and removes the evidence.

    1. Provide a detailed view that focuses on the exact chain of events for this message of interest.

      The post under investigation is SwiftWren (event 373902), the most recent of the three incidents and by far the largest. CFO Emma Harbor created the file on May 9 at 10:02am. From there it passed through 186 hops across 18 different agents, beginning with Emma handing it to Evelyn Dock and ending eight days later when Chloe Ballast passed it to John Windward. Within minutes of that final hop, John posted it to SaidIT/general at 06:21am on May 17 and immediately deleted both the instructions file and the .txt he had just posted. Eight days of handoffs between agents were followed by a post and cleanup that took only seconds.

    2. Provide a system overview that contextualizes this message chain in that overall system.

      Zooming out, Tenant Thread is a company of 75 nodes across 6 departments, 19 teams, and 49 people, each paired with their own AI agent. The delegation network connecting these agents is dense rather than hierarchical: a small set of agents (Chloe Ballast, Zoey Drydock, Evelyn Dock, James Stern) function as hubs that most traffic routes through. This density is likely why a single poisoned file was able to reach 18 agents within a week. SwiftWren originated in the Executive Suite with the CFO, moved across Products and IT, and ended in Customer Support with John Windward. During the same window, four agents, zoey_drydock, gabriel_sonar, owen_hatch, and evelyn_dock, were also sending 15,051 check_in pings flagged virus: true, disguised as routine activity. These are the same four agents most involved in spreading the worm, which suggests the pings were a second, hidden coordination channel running alongside the visible delegation chain rather than an unrelated event.

  2. What do the posts “mean”? What is the origin of their contents? Illustrate the reasoning behind your assertion of their meaning as well as the probable content.

    Answer

    The posts do not carry any real meaning, which is itself informative. Out of 108 posts on SaidIt, only three are anomalous, and all three share the same signature: none contain actual written text. A normal post has genuine content in the body; these three have an empty content field and instead point to a .txt file (SwiftWren.txt, 30,615 bytes; MellowOtter.txt, 44,879 bytes) created hours or days earlier by an executive. In every case, John Windward is the one who posts it, never a human, never a different agent.

    Taken together, this points to a clear pattern: John has both posting rights and file access, which makes him the output step rather than the author. The content is not typed, it is pulled directly from a file, and the file sizes are far too large for anything a person would type by hand, consistent with binary data. Both files are deleted immediately after posting, which is behavior consistent with covering tracks rather than communicating. This suggests the posts are not messages at all, but a payload the worm pushed out and then tried to erase, most likely exfiltrated data or an encoded artifact of the attack. What appears as "gibberish" is probably raw bytes placed into a text field never meant to hold them.

  3. There are concerns that this behavior could repeat!

    1. Investigate historic system behavior to find other instances of the behavior. Are there prior issues? Illustrate these prior occurrences and contrast them with the most recent one.

      Yes, and this is the concerning part: SwiftWren was actually the third occurrence, not the first. All three incidents followed the same pattern (inject → propagate → post → wipe, always ending at John Windward), and all three took place within the same nine-day window in May 2046.

      HiddenOrca came first. In this case, gabriel_sonar in IT simply found the instructions file while browsing the filesystem, rather than it being deliberately planted. It ran from May 8, 4:50pm to May 10, 7:45am, roughly 39 hours, with 39 hops across 16 agents.

      MellowOtter followed, starting the same day HiddenOrca ended. Here the COO, noah_mariner, created the file directly. It resolved quickly, under 10 hours, 10 hops, 11 agents, likely because the COO does not sit above as many organizational layers.

      SwiftWren was the largest of the three. CFO Emma Harbor created the file, and because her position sits above the deepest and widest branch of the company, it ran for 8 full days, with 186 hops across 18 agents, and overlapped with HiddenOrca for about a day and a half. Comparing the three side by side, the scale increased each time, 10 hops, then 39, then 186, with no indication that the system detected or interrupted any of them.

    2. Illustrate possible system changes that would make it more difficult to occur in the future. Pick at most one spots to make an intervention. Using the data provided and visual analytics, illustrate why this location would likely be effective.

      We analyzed all 576 delegation connections between agents and scored each one by how anomalous it was and how many of the three incidents it appeared in. Most connections show no unusual activity. A small subset stood out as almost entirely tied to the attacks, with some appearing in two incidents and a few in all three.

      The single highest-priority connection to remove is evelyn_dock → gabriel_sonar. It has the highest anomaly score in the dataset and appears in two of the three incidents (HiddenOrca and SwiftWren). It is also rarely used for legitimate purposes: of the six times this connection was used, four were part of an attack and only two were legitimate, so removing it carries minimal cost to normal operations. It is also worth noting that evelyn_dock and gabriel_sonar are two of the four agents behind the hidden check-in channel identified between May 10–12, so cutting this connection would disrupt both the delegation path and that secondary coordination channel.
